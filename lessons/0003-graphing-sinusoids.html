Graphing Sinusoids

One equation, four independent knobs. Once you see that each of A, B, C, D moves the curve in exactly one way — and see where the exam hides a trap between them — every graph question becomes a lookup.

This is high-leverage: "Representations of Functions" is the single largest slice of the CLEP Precalculus exam (~30%), and reading or predicting a trig graph's behavior — amplitude, period, phase shift, intervals of increase — is squarely inside it. (CLEP Precalculus exam page) The released samples lean on reading graphs, not just drawing them.

Primary source for this lesson: Paul's Online Math Notes — Graphs of Trig Functions. Free and college-level; read it for more worked graphs than fit here.

Keep the sinusoids reference sheet open alongside this.

The one form to know

Every sine or cosine graph question is built on this shape:

y = A · sin(B(x − C)) + D

Four numbers, four independent effects. That independence is the whole idea — change one and exactly one feature of the curve moves:

KnobControlsRead it off as
Aamplitude — how tall|A|
Bhow fast it cyclesperiod = 2π/B
Chorizontal (phase) shiftC
Dvertical shift / midlinemidline y = D
The two couplings that cost points Two of these are not read off directly, and that's where CLEP sets its traps. The period is 2π/B, not B — a bigger B means a shorter period. And the phase shift is C only when B is factored out — in sin(2x − π) the shift is not π. Both examples below are built around exactly these.

Move one knob at a time

Before any worked example, build the intuition directly. Drag each slider and watch which single line in the readout changes. Start by moving B and confirm the period readout moves the opposite way — bigger B, smaller period.

Now build one yourself

The grapher below has a hidden target curve drawn as a faint dashed line. Move the sliders until your curve lands on it and the readout confirms a match. Read the target's amplitude, period, midline, and shift off its shape first, then set the knobs — don't just wiggle.

sin vs cos is just a phase shift cos is sine shifted left by π/2: cos x = sin(x + π/2). So any cosine graph can be written as a sine graph and vice versa — if a problem's answer choices are all sines but the graph looks like cosine, don't panic, just shift. Toggle the grapher between sin and cos to watch the curve slide by a quarter period.
