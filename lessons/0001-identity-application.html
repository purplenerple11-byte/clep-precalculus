Applying Trig Identities

You already know the identities by name. This lesson is about the move from "I recognize that" to "I know which one to reach for."

You've told me the unit circle is solid and you recognize the Pythagorean, sum/difference, and double-angle identities — but application is rusty. That's a very normal gap: identities are easy to verify when someone hands you the answer, and hard to select when you're staring at a blank problem. This lesson closes that gap with three worked examples and a strategy you can reuse on any CLEP identity or equation question.

The core strategy

Every CLEP identity-application problem — whether it says "simplify," "verify," or "solve" — responds to the same short checklist:

  1. Rewrite everything in terms of sin and cos. tan, sec, csc, cot are just disguises. Converting them away removes half the confusion immediately.
  2. Look for a Pythagorean substitution. If you see sin²+cos² anywhere (even split across terms), that's your opening.
  3. If two different angle expressions appear (like θ and 2θ), collapse them to one. That's what double-angle identities are for — pick the double-angle form that leaves you with the variable you already have.
  4. Factor before you solve. Trig equations that look quadratic (in sin θ or cos θ) factor exactly like quadratics do.
  5. Respect the given interval, and check for extraneous roots if you squared anything along the way.
