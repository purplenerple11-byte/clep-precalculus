The Triangle Inside the Ratio

One ratio is never just one fact. Given sin θ = z/10, you can recover every other trig value of θ — by drawing the triangle the ratio is secretly describing.

This is the move CLEP tests constantly without ever naming it: you're handed a single ratio — sin θ = z/10, tan θ = 2/3 — and asked for a different function of the same angle. No calculator can do this for you in Section 2. The three-step recipe: draw the triangle the ratio describes, Pythagoras the missing side, read off what's asked.

Primary source for this lesson: Paul's Online Math Notes — Inverse Trig Functions (the worked compositions there use exactly this triangle construction), together with your right-triangles reference.

The ratio is a blueprint

sin θ = z/10 says: there is a right triangle where the side opposite θ is z and the hypotenuse is 10. That's a full blueprint — Pythagoras fills in the third side:

adjacent = √(10² − z²) = √(100 − z²)

Everything from lesson 0002 still applies — this is the same setup checklist, run in reverse: instead of measuring a triangle to get a ratio, you unfold a ratio back into a triangle.

When θ isn't acute — the sign step

The triangle only hands you magnitudes. If the problem places θ outside QI, you keep the triangle's numbers and set the sign from the quadrant.
