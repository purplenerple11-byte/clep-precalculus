Counting Solutions on an Interval

cos x = 1/2 has two solutions on [0, 2π). cos 2x = 1/2 has four. The inner multiplier quietly doubles the harvest — and "how many solutions?" is a question CLEP can ask without you solving anything.

You solved equations like these in lesson 0001 — but always with a plain θ inside. The moment the inside becomes 2x or 3x, most people still solve one cycle's worth and stop, losing half the roots. The fix is a substitution habit that makes the extra cycles impossible to miss.

Primary source for this lesson: Paul's Online Math Notes — Solving Trig Equations (its later examples are exactly this pattern). Companion sheet: the counting reference.

The substitution habit

  1. Name the inside: let u = 2x (or 3x, or 2x − π — whatever's inside).
  2. Stretch the window: if x runs over [0, 2π), then u = 2x runs over [0, 4π) — two full circles, not one. This is the step people skip.
  3. Solve in u, one circle at a time: find the solutions in [0, 2π), then add 2π to each for the second circle, and so on.
  4. Divide back: x = u/2 for every u you found.
The count, before any solving On [0, 2π), for a value c strictly between −1 and 1: sin(Bx) = c and cos(Bx) = c each have 2B solutions — two per circle, B circles. tan(Bx) = c has 2B solutions for any real c — tangent hits every value exactly once per period, and 2π holds 2B of its π/B-periods. The exceptions: sin or cos equal to ±1 exactly gives only B (the peak is hit once per circle), and |c| > 1 gives zero.
