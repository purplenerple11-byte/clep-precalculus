Solving Right Triangles

You already know the unit circle. A right triangle is that same picture with the hypotenuse unlocked from 1 — so this lesson extends what you have rather than restarting it.

This is worth your time even though it looks elementary. College Board states that roughly 30–40% of the exam requires right-triangle trigonometry or the properties of trig functions — far more than the 15% the "Trigonometry" heading suggests. The official outline names it explicitly: "apply the basic trigonometric ratios and identities (in right triangles and on the unit circle)." (CLEP Precalculus exam page)

The bridge from what you already know

On the unit circle, a point at angle θ has coordinates (cos θ, sin θ). Drop a vertical line from that point to the x-axis and you've drawn a right triangle: the vertical leg is sin θ, the horizontal leg is cos θ, and the hypotenuse is 1.

So the unit circle is not a different system from right-triangle trig — it's the special case where the hypotenuse equals 1. When the hypotenuse isn't 1, the ratios still hold, they just stop being bare coordinates:

sin θ = opposite / hypotenuse   ·   cos θ = adjacent / hypotenuse   ·   tan θ = opposite / adjacent

On the unit circle the denominator was 1, so it vanished and sin θ was the vertical coordinate. That's the whole difference. Everything you've memorized still applies.

Why this trips up people who know the unit circle On the unit circle there is only ever one reference angle — θ, measured from the positive x-axis. So you never had to ask "opposite to what?" In a right triangle there are two acute angles, and opposite and adjacent swap depending on which one you're referencing. The hypotenuse never moves. That relativity is the actual new idea in this lesson.

Watch the swap happen

Same triangle, same three sides. Toggle the reference angle and watch which leg earns which name. Note that the hypotenuse stays put while the legs trade roles — and that sin A = cos B, which is the co-function identity you already have on your reference sheet, appearing here as a picture.

The setup checklist

Almost every CLEP right-triangle question is really a setup question. The arithmetic is trivial once the setup is right, so put your effort here:

  1. Draw the triangle and mark the right angle first. Word problems hide it — "a vertical pole," "the level ground," "a plumb line" all mean 90°.
  2. Label what you're given and circle what you want. Literally write the numbers on the sides.
  3. Name the sides relative to the angle you're using — opposite, adjacent, hypotenuse. Do this after step 2, never before.
  4. Pick the ratio that connects what you have to what you want. This is the only real decision. Two knowns, one unknown, one ratio.
  5. Solve, then sanity-check. The hypotenuse must be the longest side; a leg can never exceed it.

Step 4 is the whole game, and it's mechanical once you see it as a lookup: which of the three ratios mentions both the side you know and the side you want?

Calculator mode — a genuine points-loser Degree mode vs radian mode silently changes every answer. sin 35° = 0.574, but sin(35 radians) = −0.428. The calculator won't warn you. CLEP mixes degrees and radians across questions, so check the mode indicator every single time the units change.

Worked example 2 — Angle of elevation

This is the applied form CLEP actually favors. The words change; the triangle doesn't.

Two definitions worth pinning down, because they're the only thing "hidden" in these problems:

  • Angle of elevation — measured up from the horizontal to your line of sight.
  • Angle of depression — measured down from the horizontal to your line of sight.

Both are measured from the horizontal, never from the vertical. That single fact resolves most confusion in this problem type.

The 45° sanity check tan 45° = 1 exactly. So for any angle of elevation under 45°, the height is less than the ground distance; above 45°, it's greater. You can eyeball whether an answer is plausible before computing it — useful in Section 2, where you have no calculator.

Worked example 3 — Find an angle (inverse trig)

Reverse the question: given two sides, find the angle. This is where inverse trig earns its keep.

Which direction are you going? Angle in, ratio out → use sin, cos, tan.  Ratio in, angle out → use sin⁻¹, cos⁻¹, tan⁻¹. Before touching the calculator, ask which of those two sentences describes your problem. The notation sin⁻¹ means "inverse sine," not 1/sin — that's csc. Confusing the two is a classic trap and CLEP does bait it.
